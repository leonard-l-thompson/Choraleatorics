C h o r a l e a t o r i c s a voice-leading and inversions practice tool
       
       
       
  • What is it ?
    A randomly generated chord sequence of triads, whose root names are a shuffled chromatic scale . Most of the exercises are designed with spread voicings in mind, where the distance between the outer two voices exceeds an octave. You can choose the set of allowable chord qualities with one of the buttons just below the sequence. The default is mostly a combination major and minor, with one diminished and one augmented thrown in.
  • What is the goal?
    Improvise short pieces or play any exercises through a sequence, employing inversions and smooth voice leading to render a connected and musical three-voice texture.
  • Some Exercises
    1. Start on any inversion of a spread voicing of the first chord. Move in either direction to the closest notes of the next chord, staggering chromatic travel of each voice, taking as much time as you need. Common tones between adjacent chords may remain stationary.
    2. Invent a melodic shape, and assign it to a single voice or trade it between voices, while striving generally for smooth and musical travel within each voice from one chord to the next. A melodic shape could be
      • Just the chord tone itself, sustained or repeated in some form
      • The chord tone precededed by an upper or lower neighbor (chromatic or whole step)
      • Anything of your own invention, for example [chord tone → upper neighbor → chord tone → lower neighbor → chord tone]
    3. Assign different melodic shapes to more than one voice.
      1. top voice: lower neighbor → chord tone → chord tone
        middle voice: repeated quarter notes
        bottom voice: sustained single tone
      2. Arpegiated compound melody with neighbor tones: Preceed each chord tone by un upper or lower neighbor. Bring out or hold any one of the voices through the sequence to create a connected melody line. Double or triple the amount of material for each chord by manifesting additional inversions before moving to the next chord
      3. Assign an arpeggiated triplet octave figuration (it ascends then descends) to the top voice. Its downbeat will be the top note of the chord. Let the middle voice be a singing melody.
      4. Have the root and third of the chord temporarily trade voices and then return, before moving on to the next chord.
      5. A two voice exercise where the top voice is sustained as a melody note, while the voice below it is an eighth note figuration accompaniment that alternates between the remaining two chord tones. This can be realized in two hands with spread voicings or in a single hand with more compact voicings.
      6. A texture modeled after Chopin's Prelude in E Minor, whereby the lower two voices sound together in rhythmic ostinato, with staggered travel, while the top voice sings a sustained melody.
      7. Outer voices are sustained tones while the middle voice plays a motivic figuration using any chord tones and approach notes
      8. Top and middle voices are appoggiaturae of contrary motion and different speeds
      9. A spread-voicing arpeggiation texture modeled after Bach's G Major cello suite. (effectively a single-voice exercse).
      10. A two voice exercise where the top voice is an octave triplet arpeggiation (up, then down) of the chord and the bottom voice is a soaring melody that centers around chord tones.
      11. Top voice is an ostinato repeating the chord tone, while the middle voice is is free melody that still accentuates its chord tone.
    4. Add a fourth free voice guided by your ear. One option is adding a sustained bottom voice, continuing to experiment with any of the aformentioned melodic shape exercises in the remaining three voices. Another option is a freely improvised melodic top voice allowing for either sustained chords below, or an Alberti rendering of the bottom three-voices (the latter effectively a two voice exercise).
    5. Inversions "flash card" practice. Moving through the sequence, play all three spread-voicing inversions of a chord before moving on to the next chord. Precede each chord with the lower chromatic neighbor of the top voice to add interest. Switch direction rangewise as desired to imply larger melodic contours.
    6. Play an agnostic version of the exercise: Pick any quality from one chord to the next, or try a single quality for an entire sequence. Or, play any chord as long as the root name appears somewhere in that chord. Play four-voice versions of any of the aformentioned, with the added free voice in the bass guided by your ear.
    7. Have fun inventing your own.
  • Thoughts
    • When encountering consecutive chords whose root names are relatively close, become comfortable moving range-wise in the direction opposite to what the root names might seem to suggest . This will produce two different inversions, allowing the avoidance of paralell structure when desired. This principle is wonderfully illustrated in Mick Goodrick's "Mr. Goodchord" books. For instance:
      • C♯mi→Dma: move the voicing down rather than up.
      • Fma→E♭ma: move the voicing up rather than down.
    • Shape sequences into smaller musical phrases (the three rows as they appear on screen can work well), allowing pauses or temporary suspension of tempo between each.
    • The more challenging aspect in many of the exercises will be determining the unspecified passing material between chords. Keeping the good counterpoint aesthetics in mind and staggering the movement of each voice will yield much extra harmonic content beyond what is explicit in the chord sequence.
    • Using a pool of 4 chord qualities, there are over 2.8 billion distinct sounding Choraleatorics.
